Introduction

Pampanga is known as the culinary capital of the Philippines due to its mouthwatering traditional cuisine and vibrant culinary tradition. Some of the dishes from the Kapampangan region, such as sisig, bringhe or paella, and Burong Kapampangan, not only left their stamp on the local culinary scene but also helped the nation become known for its cuisine internationally. Whenever one thinks of going on a food rampage, Pampanga always tops the list. Spanish friars and sailors taught Kapampangans the fundamentals of Spanish cooking while the Philippines was ruled by Spain. The technique of sangkutsa (precooking) and food preservation, which Kapampangans are known for, demonstrates their attention to detail when it comes to food. The creativity, kindness, and adventurous nature of the Kapampangan are what give rise to Kapampangan cuisine. Instead of simply cooking what the Spaniards taught them, Kapampangan cooks create, reinvent, and sometimes even improve each meal. Aside from creating its own original recipes, Pampango cookery also manages to create its own take on the most recent cuisine trend using fresh products. The process of cooking and preparing seems simple. It is also best done with a dash of love, just like with any cooking.

Burong Kapampangan, otherwise called tag-ilo if fish is the main ingredient used and balo-balo if shrimp is used in the fermentation is one of the foods that Pampanga is renowned for. A cuisine called Burong Kapampangan has been handed down through the centuries. The Tagalog term buro means to be preserved, fermented, or pickled. Due to the fact that burong kapampangan is made from fermented rice, it resembles porridge. The flavor is salty and sour with a hint of sautéed garlic. The buro smells magnificent when it is cooked. This dish is not your typical dish, for its taste is out of this world. This dish is easy to make. It will surely satisfy your cravings. The ingredients of this dish are very affordable, it is not too expensive. Burong Kapampangan, this dish will never fail you. Buro, as the Kapampangans refer to balo-balo, is another name for it. The balo balo is made up of fermented rice and fresh, live shrimp sautéed with garlic. It is excellent paired with grilled talong (eggplant), grilled or fried hito, and fresh mustard leaves (mustasa). It takes some getting used to, but once the palette is won over by the sour, you’ll surely love it.

HOW FERMENTATION HAPPENS AND OCCURS IN BURONG HIPON?

Fermentation is a process that occurs when microorganisms such as bacteria or yeast convert carbohydrates into alcohol or acids. In the case of burong shrimp, the fermentation process is initiated by the bacteria and yeast present in the rice wine or coconut water, which consume the sugars in the liquid and produce alcohol and lactic acid. The alcohol and lactic acid help to preserve the shrimp, giving it a distinct sour and slightly spicy flavor. The fermentation process also changes the texture of the shrimp, making it softer and more pliable. The length of the fermentation process will depend on the desired level of sourness and the desired texture of the shrimp, and can range from a few days to several weeks.
